Key Findings
⚡
Wild Pitch Compound Effect
At 1.33 WPs per PA, a runner on base almost always advances. Getting on base by ANY means — hit, HBP, error — is worth roughly 1.3 automatic base advances. First-inning runners score at an extraordinary rate.
🔗
Bird + Day Are a Chain Reaction
Day's .429 OBP + 13 stolen bases means he's on 2nd before Bird even swings. Bird's .565 OBP with 3 HR means Day scores nearly every time Bird reaches. Separating them costs ~2 runs/game in simulation.
📈
Williams Was Underrated
Season card showed .238 AVG. Real box score OBP is .391 — placing him as the 3rd best on-base threat on the team. He belongs in the top 3 of the batting order, not buried mid-lineup.
🌀
DellaCrosse Scores Via Chaos
His R/PA of .409 nearly matches Day despite a .273 OBP. He reaches on errors, draws wild pitches, and steals without hesitation. He scores without hits — the perfect chaos agent for slots 4–6.
🚫
Never Cluster Weak Bats
Placing Hicks (.133), Cross (.154), and McGinnis (.154) in consecutive slots guarantees a 3-up-3-down inning every time through. In a 5-inning game, one dead inning can cost the game. Always interleave.
🩹
Shumate's Hidden Problem
Shumate's .316 OBP looks good, but his R/PA is only .105 — lowest on the team. He gets on base but doesn't score, suggesting he's frequently stranded or running into outs on the bases. Use behind Bird/Day, not before them.
🎯
Daniels: Appearance vs. Reality
His .154 AVG looks terrible. But 3 HBP in 16 PA gives him a real .312 OBP. In 10U kid pitch, a HBP runner advances on every wild pitch just like a hit. Don't let batting average fool you into batting him last.
🏆
Playoff Loser Bracket Edge
Loser bracket teams face weaker pitching with higher walk/wild pitch rates. The simulation's 1/3 WP model will likely be conservative — real playoff ERAs could be even higher. Speed + OBP compounds more as pitching quality drops.
